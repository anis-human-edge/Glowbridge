## **1\. THE CORE IDEA**

Build a category-focused platform that helps **up-and-coming product brands** from selected source countries connect with **up-and-coming influencers** in selected target markets, starting with a single category and a single strong market wedge.

This is not a platform for the biggest brands or the biggest creators.

The most overlooked commercial opportunity sits in the gap between rising products and rising influencers.

Large brands already have agencies, budgets, distribution, and celebrity access. Top influencers already have deal flow, inbound brand interest, and pricing power. The real opening is the under-served middle:

* Good but underexposed product brands  
* Credible but not-yet-famous influencers  
* Real consumers who want trusted discovery before the market gets crowded

The platform exists to make these three groups meet, learn, test, review, collaborate, and eventually transact.

---

## **2\. THE PROBLEM WE ARE SOLVING**

### **2.1 The brand problem**

Today, early-stage or emerging product brands face a painful go-to-market problem:

* They struggle to get discovered outside their home country  
* They do not know which creators are worth working with  
* They cannot easily create repeated exposure in a new market  
* Running their own launch events is expensive (£10-20K minimum per event), fragmented, and low-leverage  
* Even if they do run their own event, it won't be interesting because they have no existing audience  
* They lack trusted local social proof and consumer feedback

### **2.2 The influencer problem**

Emerging influencers face a different but related problem:

* They want access to interesting new products before everyone else  
* They want more credibility in their niche  
* They want meaningful relationships, not random one-off sponsorships  
* They want a path to grow status, access, and monetisation  
* They rarely meet suppliers directly in a structured, repeatable way  
* They "live in a fancy world and will never ever meet with an actual supplier" without a platform creating that bridge

### **2.3 The consumer problem**

Real users are left with a noisy market where discovery is dominated by whoever spends the most, not necessarily whoever offers the most interesting value.

### **2.4 The coordination gap**

* Rising brands cannot efficiently access rising creators  
* Rising creators cannot efficiently access rising brands  
* Real consumers do not get a trusted discovery layer

This platform is designed to solve that coordination gap.

## **3\. THE FOUNDATIONAL THESIS**

Five core beliefs:

### **3.1 Emerging beats saturated**

The next breakout products and creators are often more valuable to connect than the already-famous ones because they are hungry, accessible, open to partnership, willing to experiment, and can grow together.

### **3.2 Curation beats chaos**

A raw marketplace is weak. A curated discovery engine is stronger. The platform should not try to show everything to everyone. It should create structured trust through selective exposure.

### **3.3 Repeated interaction beats one-off matchmaking**

The value is not in introducing people once. The value is in creating repeated weekly and monthly rituals where products are introduced, influencers discuss and review them, people learn from one another, and partnerships form naturally over time.

### **3.4 Community is the moat**

Any list can be copied. Any landing page can be copied. Any event format can be copied. But a living community with recurring interaction, status, trust, and feedback loops is much harder to copy.

### **3.5 London is the launch bridge**

London is strategically useful because it can act as a credibility market, a meeting hub, a physical community anchor, and a bridge between global brands and English-speaking creator ecosystems.

## **4\. THE INITIAL WEDGE**

### **Chosen wedge**

**Category:** Skincare **Target market for influencer community:** London first, then broader UK, then selected international markets **Source markets for products:** South Korea first (strongest default hypothesis), then China (co-founder access advantage), then Japan, France, others

### **Why skincare**

* Strong existing creator culture  
* Product testing and review behaviour is normal and expected  
* Discovery is social and visual  
* Communities form naturally around routines, ingredients, and results  
* Newness matters (consumers actively seek new products)  
* Country-of-origin matters (K-beauty, J-beauty are established trust signals)  
* Trust and recommendation heavily influence purchasing

### **Why South Korea first**

* Global credibility in skincare innovation  
* "K-beauty" creates built-in curiosity and narrative  
* Already has innovation associations in the category  
* Easier to explain to creators and consumers  
* China remains a strong Phase 2 option (co-founder has direct supply chain access)

### **Why not start broad**

Starting with multiple categories, many countries, and all influencer types would kill focus. The first version should be narrow enough that the market instantly understands it.

**The one-sentence test:**

We help emerging skincare brands from South Korea get discovered by emerging skincare influencers in London through curated weekly digital showcases and real-world community touchpoints.

Specific. Defensible. Understandable.

## **6\. WHAT THE PLATFORM ACTUALLY DOES**

The platform is not just a website. It is a **curated discovery and relationship infrastructure** with five functional layers:

### **6.1 Discovery layer**

A structured way to surface new and notable skincare brands/products from the chosen source market.

### **6.2 Curation layer**

A repeatable mechanism to decide which products are featured each week or month. This is one of the hardest parts of the model and must be handled carefully. (See Section 8.)

### **6.3 Event layer**

A recurring digital format where selected products are introduced to selected influencers in a structured, lightweight, high-energy format. Plus monthly physical events.

### **6.4 Community layer**

An ongoing network where influencers, hosts, reviewers, and brands interact over time rather than disappearing after one event.

### **6.5 Signal layer**

Feedback, reviews, reactions, engagement, product interest, and consumer response become signal assets that improve future curation and monetisation.

## **7\. THE CORE EXPERIENCE**

### **7.1 Weekly digital showcase**

A recurring online event, approximately 60 minutes, built around a narrow category focus.

**Purpose:**

* Introduce selected emerging products  
* Let influencers ask questions and react  
* Create peer learning among influencers  
* Generate discovery content and product understanding  
* Create momentum and recurring engagement

**Principle:** It should feel less like a sales pitch and more like a high-signal category session. The platform is not "pushing products." It is curating category discovery.

**Possible structure:**

* Host welcome and framing  
* Theme of the week  
* Introduction of selected brands/products  
* Live reactions and questions  
* Influencer discussion  
* Sampling and next-step opportunities  
* Community shout-outs and future host opportunities

**Open question:** What platform hosts these? (Zoom, custom, social live?) This affects the experience and the data collected.

### **7.2 Monthly physical event in London**

This is the reality layer. Digital creates scale. Physical creates trust.

**Purpose:**

* Convert online relationships into real-world credibility  
* Let creators and suppliers meet face-to-face  
* Build identity around the community  
* Increase stickiness and prestige  
* Generate richer content and social proof

**Strategic logic:** Without physical touchpoints, the platform risks becoming just another online listing or webinar series. London-based gatherings create status, memory, and belonging.

## **8\. THE CRITICAL CURATION PROBLEM**

One of the biggest strategic risks is pretending objectivity where none exists.

The phrase **"Top 10"** sounds powerful but is dangerous if the methodology is weak. If the selection logic is vague, the platform will look rigged, commercial, or unserious.

### **The rule: We do not make claims stronger than our evidence.**

### **Recommended language hierarchy**

Use different labels depending on what can truly be defended:

| Label | Best when... |
| ----- | ----- |
| **"Curated 10"** | Selection is editorial, not statistically definitive |
| **"Most Interesting This Week"** | Freshness and discovery are the focus |
| **"Emerging Brands to Watch"** | Credibility without pretending absolute ranking |
| **"Featured Discovery List"** | Goal is exposure rather than hard ranking |
| **"Community-Validated Standouts"** | Enough reviews and engagement data exist (later stage) |

### **Key principle**

At the beginning, lean on: curated, selected, featured, emerging, noteworthy.

Stay away from: top, best, number one, definitive ranking — unless the methodology truly supports it.

### **The rotation mechanism (from founder conversation)**

Products can be displaced by newcomers: "If the top 10 gets disrupted by a newcomer, that's the story." Multiple list types accommodate volume: stable curated list \+ rotating weekly discoveries \+ new and innovative entries.

### **The unresolved question**

**How exactly do we rank/select products?** Ingredient quality? Independent testing? User reviews? Sales data? Innovation score? Community votes? This is flagged as a critical unsolved problem that Domain Engineering must address. The methodology must be transparent and defensible. If it looks like pay-to-play, the entire credibility collapses.

## **9\. THE HOST IS NOT OPTIONAL**

A strong host is not decoration. The host is part of the product.

### **Why the host matters**

The host legitimises the session, creates energy and flow, prevents awkwardness, translates products into category relevance, and makes the platform feel human, credible, and alive.

### **What kind of host is needed**

Not a celebrity. Not a random presenter. Someone who is:

* Strong in personality  
* Respected in the niche  
* Curious and articulate  
* Disciplined and reliable  
* Capable of asking intelligent questions  
* Comfortable in public interaction

### **The strategic stance**

"We don't need famous people, we need strong people." "We will not talk to anybody who's already famous. We have to go to people who are not famous and make them famous but they have strong personalities."

This is consistent with the entire platform thesis: emerging, not established.

### **The host ladder (progression system)**

The platform can create internal talent development:

1. **Attendee** — shows up  
2. **Reviewer** — contributes feedback  
3. **Featured reviewer** — highlighted for quality  
4. **Guest host** — tries hosting a session  
5. **Recurring host** — regular hosting role  
6. **Category partner** — equity stake, face of the platform

"You become a host next week if you are really able to do well this week. And we'll pay you something."

## **11\. BUSINESS MODEL**

### **Primary revenue: from the supply side (brands)**

For brands, the platform is a lower-cost, repeated, community-based market-entry vehicle. Compared with running their own event or local activation, this is cheaper and more scalable.

**Early monetisation possibilities:**

* Featured placement fees  
* Participation fees for selected brand showcases  
* Subscription access for brands to the platform/community  
* Sampling coordination fees  
* Premium feedback/reporting packages  
* Event sponsorship  
* In-person activation packages

### **Critical caution**

Do not monetise too aggressively too early if trust is still fragile. The platform must balance two truths:

1. Brands are the first obvious payer  
2. Curation must remain visibly credible

If brands believe placement is purely pay-to-play, credibility collapses.

### **The chicken-and-egg problem**

* Suppliers won't pay unless there are influencers  
* Influencers won't come unless there are interesting products  
* Solution: start FREE for suppliers to build initial supply, then monetise once the community has momentum  
* "We will not charge you any money to work with us, only if we select you"

### **Investment stance**

* "Nobody will invest in this" at the current stage — and that's a design choice  
* Prove it first, then seek investment  
* £100K mentioned as a potential future raise once traction exists  
* Bootstrap-first mentality: "let's run it and see what happens"

## **15\. THE CORE STRATEGIC TENSION**

The business sits inside a hard but productive tension:

**It must be open enough to attract participants BUT curated enough to preserve quality and trust.**

If too open: quality drops, signal gets noisy, brand becomes generic. If too closed: growth slows, network effects weaken, discovery becomes too narrow.

The platform must behave like a **porous filter**: easy to apply to, selective in what gets featured, transparent enough to build trust.

## **18\. THE RISKS**

### **Risk 1 — Fake curation**

If featured products are obviously pay-driven or arbitrary, trust dies.

### **Risk 2 — Weak participation loop**

If influencers attend once and never return, the network never compounds.

### **Risk 3 — No measurable brand value**

If brands cannot see outcomes (content produced, engagement generated, market traction), they will not pay repeatedly.

### **Risk 4 — Host weakness**

A mediocre host makes the whole format feel second-rate.

### **Risk 5 — Premature category expansion**

Expanding before mastering skincare would dilute identity.

### **Risk 6 — Overclaiming**

Language that exceeds evidence will damage credibility.

### **Risk 7 — Over-engineering too early**

The first moat is not software. It is curation, rhythm, trust, and outcomes.
